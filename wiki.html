National AI Governance Playbook — Overview

National AI Governance Playbook for Global Majority Governments

A resource-constrained Global South government can establish credible, internationally benchmarked AI governance in 12–18 months without passing any new AI law — using only existing procurement rules, data-protection statutes, and executive circulars. See zero-new-legislation-pathway.

The Diagnostic Anchor (UNESCO RAM Execution)

← Back to Playbook

The Diagnostic Anchor

Package component #1. Complete the UNESCO RAM to secure an evidence-based roadmap and validate national strategic priorities within ~12 weeks.

What it is

The diagnostic foundation of the overview sequence. A government runs the unesco-ram — a free, UNESCO-facilitated, cross-ministerial self-assessment — to produce a scored national AI readiness profile and a prioritized action matrix. Paired with extraction of the country's four imf-ai-preparedness-index dimension scores, this gives a data-driven picture of strategic deficiencies at near-zero cost.

Why no new law is needed

It is a diagnostic, not a regulation — requested through the National Commission for UNESCO and run by existing ministry staff. Nothing is legislated; the output simply feeds the National AI Strategy.

Transferable mechanism (steps)

  1. Request UNESCO field-office facilitation (free).
  2. Convene a 5–8-person core team across ICT, Finance, Justice, and the data authority.
  3. Complete the RAM questionnaire over 2–3 working sessions (2023 facilitation guide).
  4. Run one multi-stakeholder validation workshop (civil society + private sector).
  5. Extract IMF Regulation-&-Ethics sub-scores to triangulate weakest dimensions.
  6. Publish the prioritized matrix as the evidence annex to the National AI Strategy.

Trigger / timing

Diagnose phase, Months 1–9. Low-income countries have completed the RAM in as little as 6 weeks; the user's 12-week target is comfortable.

The Procurement Lever (AIA Ministerial Circular)

← Back to Playbook

The Procurement Lever

Package component #2. Issue a Ministerial Circular requiring an Algorithmic Impact Assessment (AIA, adapted from Canada's open-source tool) for every public-sector algorithmic deployment.

What it is

The accountability backbone. A finance/treasury circular makes the canada-directive-adm AIA — a free, MIT-licensed, web-based questionnaire — mandatory before any ministry deploys or procures an AI system. The AIA auto-scores each system into one of four impact levels, each triggering proportionate obligations (notice → explanation → peer review/audit → independent bias audit).

Why no new law is needed

The AIA operates under existing procurement and budget-approval authority — exactly how Canada does it (no statute; Treasury Board authority). See zero-new-legislation-pathway. For the highest levels, attach an eu-ai-act Article 26 conformity clause to the contract.

Transferable mechanism (steps)

  1. Translate the open-source AIA questionnaire (MIT licence) into the national language.
  2. Issue the ministerial circular requiring an AIA before deployment/procurement.
  3. Stand up a simple public AIA registry webpage (system name, impact level, date).
  4. Use Level III–IV obligations as minimum high-stakes contract clauses.
  5. Designate one trained focal-point officer to review submissions.

Trigger / timing

Anchor phase, Months 6–18. Pairs with the regulatory-lever and the redress-focal-point.

The Regulatory Lever (Data Authority Guidance Note)

← Back to Playbook

The Regulatory Lever

Package component #3. Publish a Data Protection Authority Guidance Note (adapted from Brazil's ANPD) clarifying that existing data-privacy laws protect citizens against unchecked automated profiling and decisions.

What it is

A guidance note issued by the national data protection authority that interprets the country's existing data-protection statute to cover automated decision-making — establishing the right to human review and to an explicit reason for automated profiling decisions. The model is brazil-lgpd-anpd (LGPD Article 20 + ANPD Resolution 16/2024).

Why no new law is needed

It uses the DPA's administrative interpretive authority over a law already on the books — most African and Latin American countries had a data-protection law by 2024. No legislation; the note clarifies how existing rights apply to algorithms. See zero-new-legislation-pathway.

Transferable mechanism (steps)

  1. Confirm the existing data-protection law contains accuracy/transparency/purpose-limitation rights.
  2. Draft the guidance note applying those rights to automated decisions and profiling.
  3. Mandate the right to human review and an explanation on request.
  4. Issue a DPIA template (modelled on ANPD Resolution 16) for high-risk government AI.
  5. Optionally request technical cooperation from ANPD/SERPRO via South-South channels.

Trigger / timing

Anchor phase, Months 6–18. Complements the redress-focal-point — the human-review right needs an intake channel to be exercised.

The Redress Focal Point (National Incident Point)

← Back to Playbook

The Redress Focal Point

Package component #4. Appoint a small National Incident Point within an existing agency, using the pre-established OECD 4-category taxonomy.

What it is

A lightweight complaints-and-incident channel hosted inside an existing body — a national ombudsman, consumer-protection agency, or the data authority — that intakes AI-harm reports and classifies them with the oecd-ai-incidents-monitor four-category taxonomy (accuracy / safety / rights / security failures). This is the redress endpoint the regulatory-lever's human-review right depends on.

Why no new law is needed

It requires only a designated email, an intake form, and a classification process inside an agency that already has a complaints mandate. No new institution, no statute. See zero-new-legislation-pathway.

Transferable mechanism (steps)

  1. Designate an existing body as national AI incident focal point.
  2. Publish a complaints form (an email address suffices at first).
  3. Adopt the OECD four-category taxonomy in all communications.
  4. Optionally require AIID searches pre-procurement and 48-hour vendor incident notice (see ai-incident-database-nist).
  5. Report annually to the OECD AI Monitor — an international accountability record at zero cost.

Trigger / timing

Anchor phase, Months 6–18 (the report's "basic public incident complaints channel").

The Infrastructure Safeguard

Package component #5. Embed a DPI Open-Standards Test into the evaluation criteria for all incoming foreign development-finance digital infrastructure proposals.

What it is

A four-criterion screen — derived from India's india-dpi-stack — applied to any development-financed digital/AI infrastructure proposal to prevent vendor lock-in before signature. See vendor-lock-in-dpi-test for the full criteria table. Reinforced by requiring DPGA-registry status (undp-dpg-standard) and GCF-model conditionality clauses (green-climate-fund).

Why no new law is needed

It is an evaluation criterion inserted into existing procurement/appraisal processes — a scoring rule, not a statute. See zero-new-legislation-pathway.

Transferable mechanism (steps)

  1. Add the four-criterion DPI Test (API openness, data portability, interoperability, governance) to infrastructure tender evaluation.
  2. Flag any proposal failing 2+ criteria for a mandatory lock-in risk review.
  3. Require DPGA-registry status or an equivalent open-standards assessment.
  4. Insert data-portability + exit-planning clauses (migration docs + APIs within 90 days) — see world-bank-procurement.

Trigger / timing

Protect phase, Months 12–24 — applied to incoming foreign development-finance proposals and paired with eu-ai-act conformity clauses in high-stakes contracts.

The Protection Survey (Exclusion Trackers)

← Back to Playbook

The Protection Survey

Package component #6. Add 5–7 algorithmic-impact questions to the next scheduled national household or economic survey to monitor localized marketplace anomalies and workforce-displacement impacts over time.

What it is

A monitoring instrument that embeds a small set of standardized questions — modelled on kenya-finaccess — into an existing national survey, paired with the ilo-automation-methodology Automation Exposure Index for sector-level displacement tracking. Together they form an early-warning system for AI-driven exclusion and labor displacement.

Why no new law is needed

It is a survey module added to an instrument that already runs (LSMS/DHS/LFS or a financial- inclusion survey). The module questions and technical assistance are free from the ILO/World Bank.

Transferable mechanism (steps)

  1. Add 5–7 questions to the next scheduled survey: Did a machine decide? Were you given a reason? Could you appeal? Did an AI tool replace your extension officer? Was an in-person service replaced by a digital-only process?
  2. Embed the World Bank STEP task-content module to capture informal-sector exposure (see world-bank-step).
  3. Cross-tabulate with the ILO AEI for the three highest-risk government sectors.
  4. Track year-on-year: a shift without improving explanation/appeal access signals governance deterioration. AEI rise >0.05 in a sector over 12 months triggers a labor-market-impact review.

Trigger / timing

Monitor phase (ongoing). Produces the domestic impact dossiers used in the Engage phase (ca5-multilateral-negotiating-playbook).

The Zero-New-Legislation Pathway

← Back to Playbook

The Zero-New-Legislation Pathway

The report's central, recurring finding: a resource-constrained government can stand up credible AI governance in 12–18 months without passing any new AI law, relying entirely on three existing sources of authority.

The three existing authorities

  1. Procurement rules — embed the AIA and EU-AI-Act conformity clauses into tender documents (the procurement-lever, canada-directive-adm, eu-ai-act).
  2. Data-protection statutes — issue a DPA guidance note applying existing rights to automated decisions (the regulatory-lever, brazil-lgpd-anpd).
  3. Executive circulars / ministerial decrees — assign governance to existing bodies and adopt model templates (the redress-focal-point, rwanda-ai-policy, au-continental-ai-strategy).

Why it works

The IMF's imf-ai-preparedness-index shows the Regulation-&-Ethics gap is narrower than the infrastructure gap — governance is achievable without digital-infrastructure parity. Rwanda (ministerial decree, <12 months), Brazil (DPA resolution), and Singapore (voluntary framework + GovTech procurement) each prove a different route.

Vendor Lock-In & the DPI Open-Standards Test

The mechanism behind the infrastructure-safeguard. Derived from India's india-dpi-stack, the DPI Test is a four-criterion check applied to any development-financed digital/AI infrastructure proposal before contract signature.

The four criteria

CriterionPro-DPI (low lock-in)High lock-in risk
API opennessOpen, published, government-ownedProprietary, vendor-controlled
Data portabilityGovernment owns data, can exportVendor cloud, export fees/restrictions
InteroperabilityOpen standards (W3C, ISO, ITU)Proprietary data formats
GovernanceStatutory body, multi-stakeholderSingle vendor / bilateral donor condition

Any proposal failing 2+ criteria triggers a lock-in risk review before signature.

Reinforcing instruments

  • undp-dpg-standard — require DPGA-registry status or an equivalent 9-criteria assessment.
  • world-bank-procurement — data-portability + exit-planning clauses (migration + APIs in 90 days).
  • green-climate-fund — conditionality clauses keeping technology choice with the borrower.

CA5 — Shifting Multilateral Negotiating Language

Capability-area hub, and the target of the Engage phase. It also serves as the multilateral-negotiating-playbook referenced from the landing sequence.

The precedents

  • doha-declaration (2001) — rewriting treaty interpretation with domestic impact data + a single shared coalition text + human-rights reframing.
  • nagoya-protocol (2010/2014) — making benefit-sharing binding via domestic law as leverage and intersessional coalition maintenance.

Capability-area hub. Regional pooling offers cost efficiencies (collective procurement, shared platforms) but requires patience — these are the furthest-advanced examples.

UNESCO Readiness Assessment Methodology (RAM)

Type: framework / diagnostic tool Origin: UNESCO (Paris HQ, CI/ICT Division) Year: 2021 (methodology); 2023 (updated facilitation toolkit) Capability area: ca1-readiness-assessment (CA1-BP1)

What it is

A government-led, facilitated self-assessment structured around four pillars: (1) Legal & Regulatory Framework; (2) Sociocultural Infrastructure; (3) Economic Capacity & Research; (4) Technological & Digital Infrastructure. Ministry representatives score 5–8 sub-indicators per pillar via a structured questionnaire, then validate them in a multi-stakeholder workshop. UNESCO field offices provide a trained facilitator at no cost. Output: a scored heatmap + prioritized action matrix that feeds a National AI Strategy.

Documented outcomes

  • Ghana's RAM (2022) directly shaped its National AI Policy (2023).
  • Senegal's RAM (2023) produced a 47-page readiness report used at Global Digital Compact consultations.
  • Paraguay's RAM flagged data-infrastructure deficit, prompting an ICT-budget reallocation decree.
  • 27 countries completed RAM by end-2023; 15 more in progress by mid-2024. Low-income countries have finished in as little as 6 weeks with a 5–8-official core team.

Transferable mechanism

  1. Request UNESCO field-office facilitation (free) via the National Commission for UNESCO.
  2. Convene a 5–8-person core team across ICT, Finance, Justice, and the data authority.
  3. Complete the questionnaire over 2–3 sessions using the 2023 facilitation guide.
  4. Run one multi-stakeholder validation workshop.
  5. Publish the prioritized matrix as the evidence annex to the National AI Strategy.

Primary source

UNESCO, Readiness Assessment Methodology (RAM) for AI (2021/2023) — https://www.unesco.org/en/artificial-intelligence/recommendation/ram (verify-before-citing — see ai-governance-global-majority-report)

Oxford Insights Government AI Readiness Index (GARI)

Oxford Insights Government AI Readiness Index (GARI)

Type: external benchmark index Origin: Oxford Insights (UK), with IDRC (Canada) Year: annual since 2017; cited edition 2023 Capability area: ca1-readiness-assessment (CA1-BP2)

What it is

An annual ranking of 193 countries across three pillars — Government (strategy, leadership, infrastructure, data, ethics); Technology Sector; Data & Infrastructure. Built from public ITU, World Bank, and academic data. The 2023 edition added a capacity-adjusted score separating structural constraints from governance intent — useful for low-income countries demonstrating ambition despite resource gaps. Unlike unesco-ram it requires no government self-completion.

Documented outcomes

  • Cited in 40+ national AI strategies as the external improvement benchmark.
  • Rwanda used its 2023 GARI scores (52nd overall, top in Sub-Saharan Africa) as a negotiating credential with the EU and Singapore — see rwanda-ai-policy.
  • Enables free peer-country analysis: find countries with similar structural profiles but higher Government-pillar scores to identify which policy moves drove improvement.

Transferable mechanism

  • Download the annual report + data file (free).
  • Pick peer comparators (same income group, similar connectivity).
  • Map the gap on Government-pillar sub-indicators (strategy, leadership, ethics) — the dimensions most controllable through policy independent of GDP.
  • Use the peer-gap analysis as the internal briefing for a ministerial AI governance roadmap.

Primary source

Oxford Insights, Government AI Readiness Index 2023 — https://oxfordinsights.com/ai-readiness/ai-readiness-index/ (verify-before-citing — see ai-governance-global-majority-report)

IMF AI Preparedness Index

Type: macro-economic readiness index Origin: International Monetary Fund, Research Department Year: January 2024 (Staff Discussion Note SDN/2024/001) Capability area: ca1-readiness-assessment (CA1-BP3)

Global Digital Compact (2024) — First AI Text Shaped by the G77

Type: UN multilateral instrument Origin: UN General Assembly (Summit of the Future); G77+China, African Group, CARICOM leads Year: adopted September 2024 (A/RES/79/L.1) Capability area: ca5-multilateral-negotiating-playbook (CA5-BP4)

What it is

The first major multilateral AI governance text where the G77 played a documented, substantive role. Specific Global South contributions in the final text: (1) "development-oriented AI" framing (African Group); (2) interoperability over standardization for DPI (ASEAN, CARICOM); (3) capacity-building as a right, not a gift (G77); (4) data-governance equity — a Nagoya-style benefit-sharing provision for AI trained on developing-country data (AU Commission).

Documented outcomes

  • First multilateral affirmation that "developing countries must benefit equitably from AI" — now citable as established consensus.
  • Established the UN AI Office (mandate to support developing-country AI capacity) and the International Panel on AI with an equitable-geographic-representation requirement.

Transferable mechanism

  • Cite GDC language as established consensus in subsequent ITU/UNESCO/WIPO negotiations.
  • Use the IPAI representation requirement as precedent for Global South seats in technical bodies.

ILO Task-Level Automation Exposure Methodology

Type: labor-market measurement methodology Origin: ILO Research Department, Geneva Year: Working Paper No. 87 (2023); WESO 2024 update Capability area: ca6-labor-displacement-early-warning (CA6-BP1)

What it is

A task-composition analysis that (1) decomposes each occupation into tasks via the ILO/O*NET Task Content Database, (2) assigns each task an automation-probability score (0–1, updated for generative AI), (3) aggregates to an occupation-level automation exposure index, and (4) cross-tabulates with national-survey employment shares to produce sector-level displacement profiles. The 2023 paper produced the first developing-country-specific estimates.

Documented outcomes

  • 24% of Sub-Saharan African jobs have high automation exposure (clerical, admin, routine agri-processing).
  • 68% of high-risk jobs in low-income countries are held by informal workers with no retraining access.
  • Applied by Ethiopia's Ministry of Labour (2023) to the textile/garment sector.

Transferable mechanism

  1. Download the ILO task-content database and automation scores (public).
  2. Cross-tabulate with your national labor force survey (ILO offers technical assistance).
  3. Profile the three highest-risk government sectors (back-office, agricultural extension, financial services).
  4. Track the Automation Exposure Index (AEI) quarterly by sector and formality.
  5. Trigger: an AEI rise >0.05 within a sector over 12 months prompts a labor-market-impact review.

World Bank STEP Skills Survey — Automation Vulnerability Tracking

Type: household skills survey + automation index Origin: World Bank Human Development Network Year: STEP surveys 2011–present; Automation Vulnerability Index WPS10415 (April 2023) Capability area: ca6-labor-displacement-early-warning (CA6-BP2)

What it is

A household skills survey in 46+ countries with a task-content module mapping workers' actual tasks to automation vulnerability. Its distinctive value: it captures informal-sector workers excluded from establishment surveys, at the individual level. The companion ID4D dataset tracks digital-identity coverage, identifying populations excluded from AI-driven financial/social systems.

Documented outcomes

  • 16% of LMIC workers are at high automation risk (>70% task probability), concentrated in transport, manufacturing, clerical, agri-processing.
  • ~850 million people with no digital identity are both most automation-vulnerable and most excluded from digital retraining — a "double exclusion."

Transferable mechanism

  • Embed the standard task-content module into an existing household survey (LSMS/DHS/LFS) — module questions free, World Bank offers technical assistance.
  • Track three early-warning indicators annually: agricultural-extension displacement; informal credit exclusion; government back-office attrition.

Kenya FinAccess Survey — Informal-Sector AI Exclusion Monitoring

Type: national financial-inclusion survey (AI-exclusion model) Origin: Central Bank of Kenya + KNBS + FSD Kenya Year: FinAccess 2021 (AI module); FSD Africa 8-country extension 2023 Capability area: ca6-labor-displacement-early-warning (CA6-BP3)

What it is

The most sophisticated financial-inclusion instrument in Sub-Saharan Africa. Its 2021 edition added questions tracking algorithm-driven credit decisions: whether the applicant used a digital platform; whether the decision was automatic with no human review; whether a refusal explanation was given; whether appeal was possible. FSD Africa extended the method to 8 more African countries.

Documented outcomes

  • 61% of Kenyan mobile-credit borrowers received automated decisions with no human review; only 12% of those rejected got an explanation or appeal.
  • Automated rejection rates for informal-sector workers ran 2.4× higher than for formal workers across Ghana, Tanzania, Uganda, Nigeria, Senegal.
  • Data fed Kenya's National Payment Strategy and the CBK Guidance Note on Digital Credit Providers (2022) — a survey-to-regulation feedback loop.

Transferable mechanism

Add 5–7 standardized questions to any household/financial-inclusion survey: Did a machine decide? Were you given a reason? Could you appeal? Did an AI advisory tool replace your government extension officer? Has an in-person service been replaced by a digital-only process? Track annually.

Smart Africa Alliance — AU-Linked Regional Infrastructure Sharing

Type: regional digital-infrastructure platform organization Origin: Smart Africa Alliance, Kigali Secretariat Year: Kigali Declaration 2013; current phase 2019–ongoing (34 member states) Capability area: ca7-regional-infrastructure (CA7-BP1)

What it is

The most operationally advanced regional digital body in the Global South. Governance: a Board of Heads of State, an independent Secretariat (Kigali), and Technical Working Groups. Three operational instruments: the Smart Africa Cloud Marketplace (collective procurement of vetted cloud services), the Smart Africa Digital Economy Fund (SADEF) (blended finance), and a standardized MOU Template for bilateral digital cooperation.

Documented outcomes

  • Cloud Marketplace secured 30–40% pricing reductions vs direct bilateral cloud contracts.
  • SADEF mobilized $52m across 9 projects (2021–2023).
  • Rwanda, Senegal, Ghana used the MOU template for bilateral AI cooperation agreements.

Transferable mechanism — key MOU clauses

  • Data sovereignty: each party retains ownership/sovereign rights over its nationals' data; no cross-border transfer without consent.
  • Cost-sharing: allocated by GDP/population/usage as negotiated.
  • Dispute resolution: Secretariat mediation (30 days) → binding arbitration (UNCITRAL).
  • Withdrawal: 90 days' notice; full data/system migration within 180 days at no cost.

ASEAN Digital Economy Framework Agreement (DEFA)

Type: regional digital-economy treaty (in negotiation) Origin: ASEAN Secretariat, Jakarta; Digital Ministers (ADGMIN) Year: launched 2022; negotiating 2023–2024 (conclusion targeted 2025); 10 member states Capability area: ca7-regional-infrastructure (CA7-BP2)

What it is

Set to be the world's first comprehensive regional digital-economy treaty among developing/emerging economies. Eight pillars: digital trade facilitation; cross-border e-commerce; digital payments; AI governance (ASEAN Guide on AI Governance & Ethics as baseline); data flows & privacy (ASEAN CBPR); digital identities; cybersecurity; standards & interoperability (preference for open standards). The pillar structure is deliberately modular — a grouping can adopt 3–4 pillars first.

Documented outcomes

  • The ASEAN CBPR system has 9 participating jurisdictions and certified 40+ companies.
  • The 2023 ASEAN AI Governance compendium documents each member state's implemented measures.

Transferable mechanism

  • Other groupings (ECOWAS, CARICOM, SAARC) can adopt the 8-pillar architecture modularly.
  • The CBPR mutual-recognition (functional equivalence, not harmonization) is the most pragmatic model for developing-country regional data governance — respects domestic diversity while enabling cross-border data flows.

EU GAIA-X — Lessons Learned (Including Failures)

Type: federated cloud/data-ecosystem framework (cautionary model) Origin: GAIA-X Association AISBL, Brussels (Germany/France founding) Year: launched October 2020; Trust Framework v23.06 (2023) Capability area: ca7-regional-infrastructure (CA7-BP4)

What it is

A federated cloud-infrastructure and data-ecosystem framework for sovereign, interoperable European cloud services with data-portability guarantees — technical specs for "federated data spaces," Bronze/Silver/Gold sovereignty labels, a 1,300+ member association, and a compliance Clearing House. Its documented difficulties are as instructive as its successes.

Documented outcomes & failures

  • Adopted as reference architecture for 5 European industrial data spaces.
  • But only ~200 services achieved label certification by 2024 — far below targets.
  • Consensus association governance proved too slow (years, not weeks); large US cloud providers joined and diluted sovereignty requirements.
  • The national adaptation (France's SecNumCloud) — stronger sovereignty, faster national decision-making — outperformed the pan-European initiative.

  • A coalition of <15 well-briefed developing countries can rewrite consensus multilateral text — the ca5-multilateral-negotiating-playbook (Doha/Nagoya/CBDR/GDC).
