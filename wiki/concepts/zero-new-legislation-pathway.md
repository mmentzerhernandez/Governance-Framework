# The Zero-New-Legislation Pathway

The report's central, recurring finding: a resource-constrained government can stand up credible AI
governance in **12–18 months without passing any new AI law**, relying entirely on three existing
sources of authority.

## The three existing authorities
1. **Procurement rules**, embed the AIA and EU-AI-Act conformity clauses into tender documents
   (the [[procurement-lever]], [[canada-directive-adm]], [[eu-ai-act]]).
2. **Data-protection statutes**, issue a DPA guidance note applying existing rights to automated
   decisions (the [[regulatory-lever]], [[brazil-lgpd-anpd]]).
3. **Executive circulars / ministerial decrees**, assign governance to existing bodies and adopt
   model templates (the [[redress-focal-point]], [[rwanda-ai-policy]], [[au-continental-ai-strategy]]).

## Why it works
The IMF's [[imf-ai-preparedness-index]] shows the Regulation-&-Ethics gap is **narrower** than the
infrastructure gap, governance is achievable without digital-infrastructure parity. Rwanda
(ministerial decree, <12 months), Brazil (DPA resolution), and Singapore (voluntary framework + GovTech
procurement) each prove a different route.
